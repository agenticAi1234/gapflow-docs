A **super admin** operates **above** any single organization. While an **organization admin** cares about their
own members, workspaces, and subscription posture, a super admin can reason about **all tenants** on the
deployment: inspecting organizations, adjusting **Custom** resource allocations, reacting to abuse or migration
events, and curating the **global template library** through approvals.

This separation exists so that day-to-day customer admins **cannot** escalate themselves to platform god-mode by
mistake. Super admin access should be rare, audited, and protected by your identity and infrastructure policies
(database flags, internal VPN-only routes, break-glass accounts, etc.).

{/* MANUAL-CONTENT-END */}

## What a super admin can do (conceptual)

Depending on how your fork is configured, super admins may:

- Open a **super admin** console or route namespace distinct from `/w/...` workspace URLs  
- List **organizations** and open detail views for support  
- Edit **organization resources** for **Custom** plans (workflows caps, storage, members, execution time, …)  
- **Deactivate** or **reactivate** organizations when policy requires a hard stop  
- Manage the **template approval queue**: pending, approved, rejected; search by id or name; edit publishable
  metadata; leave reviewer remarks  

<Callout type="warning">
  Super admin powers are **tenant-wide**. Compromise of a super admin account is a **severe** incident—enforce MFA,
  IP allow lists, and narrow roster size wherever possible.
</Callout>

## Template approvals in depth

User-submitted templates usually follow a **governance** path:

<Steps>
  <Step>A builder **publishes** from a workflow; metadata and a frozen **snapshot** are captured.</Step>
  <Step>The submission enters **pending review** (unless your policy auto-approves—rare).</Step>
  <Step>A super admin inspects preview, metadata, categories, and optional reviewer notes.</Step>
  <Step>The template is <strong>approved</strong> (visible in Browse) or <strong>rejected</strong> with optional
    feedback to the submitter.</Step>
  <Step>Optional: super admin edits **display fields** (title, description, categories) without mutating history in
    the immutable snapshot—exact rules depend on implementation.</Step>
</Steps>

**Search** in the approval UI helps match incidents to templates: filter by **status tab** (pending / approved /
rejected) and query by **template id** or **name substring**.

<Tabs items={['Metadata vs snapshot', 'Remarks']}>
  <Tab>
    **Metadata** is what end users see on cards in **Browse templates**—friendly title, services, summary text.
    The **snapshot** is the technical graph used when someone clicks **Use template**. Super admins should avoid
    implying they “fixed” a user’s blocks if only metadata changed—clarify what was edited when communicating back.
  </Tab>
  <Tab>
    **Superadmin remarks** are optional notes to publishers—for example why something was rejected or what to
    change before resubmitting. Keep remarks professional and specific to speed up resubmission cycles.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Organization lifecycle operations

Sometimes you must **deactivate** an organization—non-payment, legal hold, data migration, or emergency response.
While deactivated:

- End users should see a clear **blocked** or **inactive org** experience  
- Background jobs may be suppressed to avoid accruing usage  

**Reactivation** restores normal use; it does not automatically undo data deleted during the inactive window—check
backup policy.

## Relation to organization admins

| Topic | Organization admin | Super admin |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Scope** | Single org they belong to | Entire deployment |
| **Templates** | Submit from own org | **Approve/reject** library entries |
| **Limits** | Works within assigned plan | Can set **Custom** resource rows |
| **Membership** | Invites users to their org | May assist cross-org support scenarios |

Org admins should **not** assume they can approve their own templates globally unless product policy says so—most
deployments require super admin eyes for **public library** exposure.

## Audit and accountability

Because super admin actions affect many users:

- Prefer **reason codes** or **internal tickets** linked to limit changes  
- Rotate **shared** super admin credentials regularly  
- Train operators on **GDPR** / data-residency implications before exporting org data
